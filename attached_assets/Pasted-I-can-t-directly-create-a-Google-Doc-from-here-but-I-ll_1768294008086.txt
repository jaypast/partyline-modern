# Product Requirements Document (PRD)  
**Product:** PartyLine Modern (working name)  
**Last updated:** 2026‑01‑13

***

## 1. Product Overview

**Vision**  
A modern, safe, voice‑only “party line” that lets adults talk to real humans when they feel lonely or just want company. People can join for free 1:1 or small‑group calls, or pay to connect with vetted companions who are compensated for their time.

**Core Characteristics**

- Voice‑only: **no video, no images, no file sharing.**
- Age restriction: **18+ only.**
- Content restrictions: **no explicit sexual content**, no escorting, no hate or harassment.
- Access methods:
  - Phone number (PSTN → platform).
  - Web/app calls (WebRTC).
- Conversation modes:
  - 1:1 (free or paid).
  - Small groups up to 5 participants (free or paid host).
- Time & pricing:
  - Free calls: up to **60 minutes per call**.
  - Post‑cap extension: **5 USD per person per additional hour**.
  - Paid companion calls: **1–5 USD per minute**, with marketplace economics.

***

## 2. Target Users & Positioning

### 2.1 Primary MVP Target

**Primary cohort:** Adults **40–75** (emphasis on 50+):

- Lonely, widowed, or socially isolated.
- Comfortable with phone calls; variable comfort with apps/web.
- Often feel they are “bothering people” if they call family or friends.

**Secondary cohort:** Adults **25–45**:

- Comfortable with web/app.
- Interested in low‑pressure, anonymous voice conversations, interest rooms, or “regular” companions.

### 2.2 Positioning Statement

> “PartyLine Modern is a safe, platonic phone and web companion line for adults who want someone to talk to—without video, explicit content, or judgment.”

Differentiation:

- Not a random video/chat roulette app.
- Not a dating/NSFW or escort service.
- Not clinical therapy or telehealth.
- Inspired by:
  - 80s/90s party lines (fun, spontaneous calls).
  - Telephone befriending and companion care programs (reliability, safety).

## 3. Business Model & Unit Economics

### 3.1 Revenue Streams

1. **Paid 1:1 calls with companions**
   - Caller price: **1–5 USD per minute**.
   - Companion share: target **40–70%** depending on price tier and seniority:
     - Example splits:
       - 1.00/min → 0.60 to companion, 0.40 to platform.
       - 3.00/min → 1.80 to companion, 1.20 to platform.
       - 5.00/min → 2.50 to companion, 2.50 to platform.
   - Platform take: roughly **30–60%** depending on configuration.

2. **Paid group rooms (up to 5 participants)**
   - Hosted by a companion.
   - Participants pay either:
     - Per‑minute rates (e.g., 0.50–2.00/min), or
     - Flat session price (e.g., 10–20 for a 60‑minute group).
   - Aggregate revenue per hour is sum of all participants’ payments; companion earns per‑hour; platform takes a commission.

3. **Free calls with paid extensions**
   - Free calls: 60 minutes per call.
   - At 55 minutes, participants get prompt:
     - “You’ve reached a free hour. Continue for another 60 minutes for 5.”
   - Each participant chooses individually:
     - Those who pay continue into extended hour.
     - Those who don’t are gracefully disconnected at the 60‑minute mark.

4. **Future: Subscriptions**
   - Bundles of minutes at a discount (e.g., 120 min/month).
   - “Regulars” features (preferred companion, priority access).
   - Possible health‑plan contracts (see insurance strategy).

### 3.2 Telephony Cost Assumptions

Using CPaaS (e.g., Twilio/Vonage/Bandwidth):

- Conservative assumption: **0.015 USD per minute per call leg**, including carrier, platform, and number costs.
- A 1:1 call has **2 legs** (caller ↔ bridge, companion ↔ bridge):
  - 60 minutes → ≈ 1.80 USD total voice cost (2 × 60 × 0.015).
- A 5‑person group has **5 legs**:
  - 60 minutes → ≈ 4.50 USD voice cost (5 × 60 × 0.015).

**Economics vs pricing:**

- 1:1 at 1.00/min:
  - Revenue/hour: 60.
  - Voice cost/hour: ~1–2.
  - Gross voice margin: ~58–59 before companion payout and other costs.
- 1:1 extension at 5/hour/person:
  - 1:1 extension revenue/hour: 10.
  - Voice cost/hour: ~1–2.
  - Gross margin: ~8–9.
- 5‑person group extension at 5/hour/person:
  - Revenue/hour: 25.
  - Voice cost/hour: ~4–5.
  - Gross margin: ~20.

**Conclusion:** Telephony costs are small relative to revenue. Profitability hinges on:

- Companion share and utilization.
- Acquisition costs for callers and companions.
- Overall operational overhead.

### 3.3 Freemium → Paid Conversion Hypothesis

- Free usage is the emotional proof: “I feel better after talking.”
- Conversion thesis:
  - 5–15% of active free users try at least one paid session in 90 days.
  - 2–5% become recurring paid users (monthly).
- Key triggers:
  - Bias toward converting people after good free experiences: “Want to talk to [Name] again?”.
  - Smooth upgrade flows when calls hit the free limit (“tap to continue at 5/hour”).

## 4. Supply Seeding & Companion Experience

### 4.1 Initial Supply Strategy

- Target 20–50 high‑quality companions for beta:
  - Recruited from:
    - Retirees, teachers, social workers, pastoral/care backgrounds.
    - People already exploring “paid to chat” side hustles but preferring a safe, platonic environment.
- De‑risk early idle time:
  - Offer guaranteed floor earnings for first X hours (e.g., 15–20/hour) during beta.
- Demand control:
  - Limit paid call slots and groups per hour/day initially to keep companions busy and ensure strong early experiences.

### 4.2 Companion Tools & Burnout Prevention

- Availability & scheduling:
  - Recurring availability blocks (e.g., Mon/Wed/Fri 5–9pm).
  - “Go online now” toggle for on‑demand.
  - Callers can see “available now” or book future slots.
- Session limits:
  - Companions can set daily and weekly caps (e.g., max 4 hours/day).
  - System prompts to take breaks after long stretches.
- Support & training:
  - Short onboarding modules:
    - Empathic listening.
    - Boundaries and “I’m not a therapist” script.
    - De‑escalation and crisis hand‑off basics.
  - Peer community:
    - Private forum or chat for companions.
    - Optional group debrief sessions.

## 5. Core Functional Requirements (MVP)

### 5.1 Onboarding & Identity

**Callers**

- Age gate: confirm **18+** with date of birth.
- Phone verification (SMS) or phone‑back verification on PSTN.
- Optional email.
- Profile:
  - Nickname.
  - Age band (18–29, 30–44, 45–59, 60+).
  - Interests (tags).
  - Preferred language.

**Companions**

- Application:
  - Basic bio and motivations.
  - Interests, languages, availability.
- Identity verification:
  - Government ID and selfie verification (where feasible).
- Background checks:
  - National criminal and sex‑offender checks in primary markets.
  - Costs recouped via small onboarding fee or first payout deduction.
- Training:
  - Mandatory acceptance of role definition and code of conduct.
  - Completion of short training modules.

### 5.2 Matching & Room Types

**Matching dimensions**

- Random: “Just connect me to someone.”
- Interest‑based: common tags (music, books, parenting, grief, expats, etc.).
- Age groups: match within selected age band or compatible bands.

**Room types**

- 1:1 free:
  - Random, strictly platonic, no companion pay.
- 1:1 paid:
  - Caller selects a companion (via profile list) or “quick pick”.
  - Dynamic per‑minute price displayed clearly.
- Group free:
  - Up to 5 participants.
  - Topic or mood based (e.g., “late night 50+,” “book lovers,” “venting room”).
- Group paid:
  - Companion‑hosted.
  - Participants pay; host earns per‑minute/hour.

**In‑call controls**

- For all:
  - Mute/unmute.
  - Leave.
  - Block/report user after call ends; simple 1–2 tap flows.
- For companions/hosts:
  - Remove/disconnect disruptive participants.
  - Mark call as “emotionally heavy” for internal support.

### 5.3 Time & Payment Mechanics

**Free calls**

- Duration: 60 minutes per session.
- Warnings at 55 minutes.
- At 60 minutes:
  - If no extension purchase, call ends gracefully.
  - Option to join another free call after a cooling‑off or daily usage limit (configurable).

**Paid calls & extensions**

- Pricing:
  - 1–5/minute for paid 1:1 and some group formats.
  - 5/hour extension for participants in free calls.
- Payment flow:
  - Web/app:
    - Card or wallet top‑up.
    - Pre‑authorization for expected session length or credit bundle.
  - PSTN:
    - Free calls accessible via phone number.
    - To pay, callers are prompted (via IVR/SMS) to go to web/app to add payment method.
    - Option for later carrier billing only after maturity and risk evaluation.

## 6. Trust, Safety & Crisis Protocols

### 6.1 Policy Framework

- Prohibited:
  - Sexual content and innuendo beyond PG‑13.
  - Dating/escort framing.
  - Harassment, hate speech, doxing, threats.
  - Any engagement with minors (platform is 18+ only).
  - Representing oneself as a licensed professional (therapist, lawyer, doctor) unless in a vetted provider program (future).
- Clear role definition:
  - Companions are friendly listeners, not clinicians or romantic partners.

### 6.2 Gray‑Area Emotional Support & Parasocial Attachments

- Training:
  - Teach companions to:
    - Use reflective listening.
    - Gently decline clinical advice requests.
    - Use boundary phrases: “I’m here as a friendly ear, not a therapist.”
- Product mechanisms:
  - Limits on repeated calls:
    - Soft caps for hours/week between the same caller and companion.
    - Prompts to rotate to other companions: “Let’s meet someone new this time.”
  - Internal flags:
    - Companions can mark conversations as emotionally heavy.
    - Platform monitors for high frequency/intensity relationships for review.

### 6.3 Crisis Escalation

- Scenario: caller expresses suicidal ideation or imminent self‑harm.
- Companion protocol:
  - Stay calm; listen and validate.
  - Avoid promising secrecy or specific outcomes.
  - Provide crisis resources for caller’s location (e.g., 988 in the US, local hotlines).
  - Encourage caller to contact emergency services or a trusted person.
- Platform support:
  - “Escalate” button for companions in app.
  - Dedicated ops channel for real‑time support (chat or on‑call specialist).
- Long‑term:
  - Formal partnerships with crisis hotlines for training and warm hand‑offs.
  - Legal review of data handling and notification responsibilities.

## 7. Technical Architecture (MVP)

### 7.1 Telephony & Media

- CPaaS first:
  - Twilio/Vonage/Bandwidth‑like provider for:
    - Phone number provisioning.
    - PSTN ingress/egress.
    - Call routing into VoIP bridges.
    - E911 and regulatory basics.
- Media:
  - Central voice bridges (mixers) for 1:1 and group calls.
  - WebRTC clients for browser/mobile connect to the same mixer infrastructure.
- Long‑term scaling:
  - Move high‑volume media to self‑hosted FreeSWITCH/Asterisk + direct SIP trunks for lower per‑minute cost, keeping CPaaS for fringe geographies or backup.

### 7.2 Security & Privacy

- Transport security:
  - Encrypted web/socket connections (HTTPS/WSS).
- PII:
  - Hash or tokenize phone numbers; avoid exposing companion or caller phone numbers.
- Recordings:
  - Default: no recording for standard calls.
  - If recording is used for moderation/training:
    - Explicit consent.
    - Clearly stated retention limits and access controls.

## 9. Long‑Term Insurance‑Friendly Strategy

### 9.1 Why Insurance Cares

- Loneliness and social isolation correlate with higher healthcare utilization and costs, especially among older adults and Medicare beneficiaries.
- Medicare Advantage and some commercial plans have begun adding non‑medical benefits targeting social isolation and companionship (e.g., Papa pals, structured phone outreach, social programs).

### 9.2 “Health Plan Edition” (B2B2C)

Create a parallel offering for health plans:

- Member identification:
  - Plan uses its own data to identify high‑risk, lonely, or isolated members.
- Access:
  - Dedicated toll‑free numbers and branded web portals.
- Staffing:
  - Companions with extra training and potentially different vetting criteria.
- Programs:
  - Scheduled 1:1 check‑ins (weekly/biweekly).
  - Themed group calls (e.g., “Coffee & chat,” “Widowed members support”).
- Payment model:
  - PMPM (per member per month) or program fees rather than claim reimbursement.
  - Outcomes‑focused contracts (engagement, self‑reported loneliness reductions, downstream utilization trends).

### 9.3 Clinical‑Adjacent Integration

- Partner with behavioral health providers:
  - Use PartyLine for warm‑line and group social sessions.
  - Licensed clinicians separately bill telehealth visits (audio/video) through existing codes.
- Data & outcomes:
  - Use standardized loneliness/depression scales (e.g., UCLA Loneliness Scale) at enrollment and intervals.
  - Track:
    - Call frequency/duration.
    - Self‑reported well‑being.
    - Plan‑supplied utilization outcomes (ER visits, hospitalizations) in pilots.

### 9.4 Product Adaptations for Insurers

- Governance:
  - Co‑develop protocols with plan medical and compliance teams.
- Features:
  - Program‑specific scripts and prompts for structured outreach.
  - Additional documentation fields for companions in health‑plan programs.
  - Opt‑in data sharing with plans, fully consented by members.

### 9.5 Monetization Impact

- Consumer marketplace:
  - Direct revenue from paid calls and subscriptions.
- Insurance channel:
  - Larger, more stable contracts.
  - Potential cross‑subsidy of consumer free tiers and platform investments.
  - Stronger defensibility once integrated into care management workflows.

## 10. Design & UX: 90s‑Inspired, Modern Accessible

### 10.1 Visual Direction

- **Aesthetic:** “Soft 90s party line” – neon accents and playful shapes, but with high readability and accessibility.
- **Color palette (example):**
  - Background: deep navy (#060815) or charcoal.
  - Primary accent: neon cyan (#00F0FF).
  - Secondary accent: electric pink (#FF4FD8).
  - Tertiary accent: soft neon yellow (#FFE866).
  - Neutral: light gray/white (#F5F5F5) for content areas.

### 10.2 UI Elements

- Buttons:
  - Subtle vertical gradient (lighter top, darker bottom).
  - Large labels like “Talk to someone now,” “Join a small group.”
- Decorative elements:
  - Low‑opacity abstract shapes (squiggles, circles, rounded rectangles) in backgrounds.
  - Occasional pixel or “CRT‑style” accent in headings.
- Typography:
  - Clean sans‑serif (system fonts) with large sizes.
  - High‑contrast text and large hit areas for older adults.

### 10.3 IVR & Voice Tone

- Friendly, slightly nostalgic script:
  - “Welcome to the Party Line. Press 1 to talk to someone now. Press 2 to join a small group.”
- Short, clear prompts; repeat options often; no complex nested menus.
