## THE HEADLINE PROBLEM (Again, But Worse Now That I See It)

**“Talk to Someone on the Phone When You Feel Lonely”**

Jay. *Jay.* You’ve put the word “lonely” in magenta. You’ve made it *larger*. You’ve essentially put a spotlight on the one word that will make people close the tab.

Here’s the psychology: The moment someone reads “When You Feel Lonely,” their brain does a rapid status calculation. *“Am I the kind of person who feels lonely? Is that my identity? Do I want to admit that to a website?”* And for most people, the answer is a protective “no.”

You’re asking for a confession before you’ve offered absolution.

**The fix:** Lead with the *experience*, not the diagnosis. “Real conversations with interesting strangers.” “Your phone, used properly.” “Talk to someone new tonight.” *Then* let them self-select into the loneliness framing deeper in the funnel once they’ve already committed psychologically.

-----

## THE TONE: Clinical Meets Crisis Hotline

Your copy oscillates between two modes:

**Mode 1: The therapy brochure.** “Safe space to talk.” “Trust isn’t given, it’s earned.” “Here if you need us.” This is the language of crisis intervention. It’s the tone you’d use for someone on a ledge.

**Mode 2: The premium service.** “Premium Companions.” “$1-5 per minute.” “Connect with your favorites.”

These two tones are *fighting each other*. You can’t simultaneously say “This is a safe space for vulnerable people in crisis” and “Our premium tier is $5/minute.” It creates cognitive dissonance. Either this is a service for people in genuine distress (in which case, charging by the minute feels exploitative), or it’s a premium experience for people who want interesting conversation (in which case, the crisis hotline language is performative and off-putting).

**Pick a lane.** I’d suggest leaning into the premium/experience framing and *removing* most of the crisis language. You’ve already got the 988 hotline reference in the footer—that’s sufficient duty of care. The rest of the site should feel more like a members’ club and less like a support group.

-----

## THE “PURELY PLATONIC” PROBLEM

You’ve included a section with a “no” symbol icon that says “Purely Platonic. This isn’t dating or anything else.”

Here’s the issue: **You’ve now made everyone think about sex.** The moment you say “This definitely isn’t a sex thing,” everyone thinks about sex. It’s the classic “Don’t think about a pink elephant” problem.

The original party lines *were* used for flirtation. Everyone knows this. By denying it so loudly, you’re actually raising suspicion rather than allaying it.

**Better approach:** Simply don’t address it. Frame the service around *conversation topics*—books, history, gardening, current events—and let the wholesome framing speak for itself. “Eleanor Wilson: Retired teacher who loves sharing stories.” That’s sufficient. No need for the prohibition sign.

-----

## PRICING: Brilliant Structure, Terrible Presentation

Your pricing architecture is actually quite clever:

- **Free tier:** 60 minutes, no credit card. Brilliant. Removes all friction.
- **Extended time:** $5/hour after the first hour. Very reasonable.
- **Premium companions:** $1-5/minute. Choose your favorites.

The *structure* is right. The *presentation* is wrong.

**Problem 1: Unit confusion.** You’re mixing dollars-per-minute with dollars-per-hour. This forces people to do math. “$1-5 per minute” sounds expensive until you realize it’s comparable to therapy rates. But people don’t do the calculation—they just feel sticker shock.

**Problem 2: The range.** “$1-5” is a 5x spread. That’s unsettling. People want to know what they’re paying. The range suggests unpredictability.

**Problem 3: Anchoring.** You’re leading with “Free” and then jumping to “$1-5/minute.” The free tier anchors people at zero, making the premium tier feel like gouging.

**The fix:** Lead with value, not price. Show the companion profiles *first*. Let people fall in love with Eleanor Wilson and her 137 calls and 5.0 rating. *Then* reveal that a call with Eleanor is $2/minute. Suddenly it’s not “paying for companionship”—it’s “getting access to Eleanor specifically.” You’ve moved from commodity to differentiated product.

Also consider: monthly subscriptions. “Unlimited calls with any companion for $99/month.” This changes the mental model entirely—it becomes a membership rather than a transaction.

## THE COMPETITIVE POSITIONING GAP

You say “This isn’t therapy, dating, or a crisis hotline.” Fine. But you never say what it *is* in aspirational terms.

Here’s what’s missing: **The positive vision.**

What does someone’s life look like *after* using PartyLine? Are they less anxious? More confident? Better at small talk? Do they have a standing Tuesday call with Eleanor where they discuss the week’s news?

Right now your copy is all *defensive positioning* (not this, not that) rather than *aspirational positioning* (imagine this).

**Consider adding:** A “Why People Call” section with specific scenarios:

- “I travel for work and hotel rooms get quiet after 8 PM.”
- “I’m retired and my kids are busy—I just want to talk about the game.”
- “I’m going through a divorce and need someone outside my friend group.”

These are *respectable* reasons to call. They give people permission to see themselves as the target audience without the stigma of “lonely.”
